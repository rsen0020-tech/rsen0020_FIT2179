Victorian Bushfires

A century of fire — and the schools in its path.

Each summer, thousands of Victorian children sit in classrooms inside the state's highest bushfire-risk zones. This page joins two public datasets — the Bushfire At-Risk Register of education facilities, and a national archive of historical fire boundaries — to answer two questions for parents, teachers and anyone living in the bush interface: where is the risk concentrated today, and what kind of fire seasons should Victoria expect?

Part 1

Where the risk lives

The Bushfire At-Risk Register is Victoria's roll-call of schools, kindergartens and early-learning centres that sit in bushfire-prone country. The 2025–26 edition lists 893 facilities, scattered across 61 of the state's 79 local government areas. The first thing the data shows is that this burden is anything but evenly spread.

Figure 1. Bushfire at-risk education facilities mapped to their LGA. Equal-area projection. Source: BARR register joined to ABS LGA boundaries.

Three shires alone — Yarra Ranges, Greater Bendigo and Nillumbik — hold almost a third of every at-risk facility in Victoria.

The Bureau of Meteorology divides Victoria into nine fire weather districts — the regions used to issue Code Red and other warnings. Just one of them, the Central district wrapping around Melbourne's forested fringe, accounts for more than half of the register on its own. Among individual councils, Yarra Ranges carries more than twice the at-risk count of any other shire.

Part 2

How the risk varies

Not every facility on the register faces the same level of danger. Each one is graded from Category 1 — the highest tier, where facilities must close pre-emptively on Code Red days — through Category 3, where exposure exists but is less acute. The mix changes sharply from one district to the next.

Risk also cuts across who runs each school. Early-childhood services dominate the register simply because there are so many of them, but the breakdown by sector shows the same risk-tier mix repeated again and again.

Part 3

A century of fire

Knowing where today's schools sit on the map is only half the story. The Historical Bushfire Boundaries dataset records the outline of every officially mapped fire in Victoria since 1903 — more than 77,000 entries: 9,130 wild bushfires, and tens of thousands of planned prescribed burns lit by land managers to reduce fuel. Plotted year by year, the bushfire record is dominated by a small number of catastrophic seasons.

Figure 7. Total area burnt by Victorian bushfires (orange) and prescribed burns (green) each year, with the most destructive seasons labelled.

Four seasons — 2003, 2006, 2009 and the 2019–20 Black Summer — account for the great majority of the area Victoria has lost to bushfire since 1950.

Average across the decades and a second pattern emerges: prescribed burning has grown into the dominant land-management activity, but it is deliberately scheduled into the safer shoulders of the year. Wild bushfires still arrive when they always have — in the hot, dry middle of summer.

Figure 8. Area burnt per decade, split between bushfires and planned burns.

Figure 9. Fires by month, side by side: two very different seasonal rhythms.

Stretching that monthly pattern across every decade in the record shows the summer fire window appearing in roughly the same shape, but with very different intensity from one decade to the next.

Part 4

The shape of individual fires

Finally, the size of a fire matters as much as its season. Most bushfires are small — small enough to contain. What makes Victoria's record so dangerous is the long tail: every few decades a single fire grows large enough to reshape the state's geography. The 1939 Black Friday fires remain the largest single footprint on record, at roughly 2.4 million hectares.

Figure 11. The distribution of recorded bushfire sizes — note the long tail.

Figure 12. The fifteen largest individual bushfires on record.

Read together, the two datasets give a stark answer: the map shows where today's children sit, and the history shows the kind of summer that can arrive at any time.

About this story

Why

Where is bushfire risk to Victorian schools concentrated today, and what kind of fire seasons should the state expect? Both questions matter for emergency policy and for the everyday decisions of parents and teachers.

Who

Written for the average Victorian — parents, teachers and anyone living in the bushfire interface. Specialised terms are introduced at first use and numbers are anchored to shires, seasons or named fires rather than statistics.

What · the data

  • Bushfire At-Risk Register (BARR) 2025–26, Victorian Department of Education — data.vic.gov.au.
  • Historical Bushfire Boundaries v2.0, Digital Atlas of Australia (Geoscience Australia) — digital.atlas.gov.au.
  • ABS Local Government Areas (ASGS 2011), filtered to Victoria and simplified for fast loading.

How · the idioms

An equal-area choropleth for geographic distribution; ranked horizontal bars for "which is largest?"; a stacked bar and two heatmaps for category and seasonal mix; an annotated multi-series line for time, with named seasons; a histogram for the long-tailed distribution of fire size. Colour is semantic: warm yellow-to-red for fire intensity, green for prescribed burning. Hover adds detail-on-demand.

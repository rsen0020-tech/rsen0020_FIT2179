Victorian Bushfires

A century of fire — and the schools in its path.

Each summer, thousands of Victorian children sit in classrooms inside the state's highest bushfire-risk zones. This page joins two public datasets — the Bushfire At-Risk Register of education facilities, and a national archive of historical fire boundaries — to answer two questions for parents, teachers and anyone living in the bush interface: where is the risk concentrated today, and what kind of fire seasons should Victoria expect?

Part 1

Where the risk lives

The Bushfire At-Risk Register is Victoria's roll-call of schools, kindergartens and early-learning centres that sit in bushfire-prone country. Being listed means a site is close enough to forest or grassland that the Department of Education can direct it to close on the most dangerous fire days. The 2025–26 edition lists 896 facilities, scattered across 61 of the state's 79 local government areas — and the first thing the data shows is that this burden is anything but evenly spread.

Insight Risk concentrates sharply in Melbourne's forested eastern fringe. Yarra Ranges — the deepest red shire — alone holds more than 17% of every at-risk facility in the state.

For thousands of Victorian children, the bushfire risk map isn't a statistic — it's the tree line at the far edge of the school playground.

The Bureau of Meteorology divides Victoria into nine fire weather districts — the regions used to issue Code Red and other warnings. Just one of them, the Central district wrapping around Melbourne's forested fringe, accounts for more than half of the register on its own. Among individual councils, Yarra Ranges carries more than twice the at-risk count of any other shire.

Insight The Central district carries 473 facilities — more than the other eight districts combined, and over four times the next-largest, Northern Country (106). Risk falls away sharply beyond Melbourne's forested fringe.

Insight Yarra Ranges holds more than twice the count of any other shire. The top three (Yarra Ranges, Greater Bendigo, Nillumbik) hold nearly a third of the entire register.

Part 2

How the risk varies

Not every facility on the register faces the same level of danger. Each one is graded from Category 1 — the highest tier, where facilities must close pre-emptively on Code Red days — through Category 3, where exposure exists but is less acute (a handful sit in an unrated Category 0). The mix changes sharply from one district to the next.

Insight The register has a steep shape: 747 facilities (83%) sit in lower-risk Category 3, 120 in Category 2, and just 24 in the most severe Category 1 — the tier that must close pre-emptively on Code Red days. Five further sites fall outside the scale in Category 0.

Insight The labelled numbers are Category 3 — the lower-risk tier that makes up the bulk of every district, peaking at 380 in Central. The highest-risk Category 1 sites are far rarer and cluster in Central (13), with only a handful across South West, North East and West & South Gippsland.

Who runs them

Risk also cuts across who runs each school. The register spans five education sectors, but two carry almost all of it: early-childhood services (474 facilities) and government schools (331) together hold roughly nine in every ten at-risk sites.

The same mix everywhere

What stays constant is the shape of each bar. Whichever sector you look at, the lower-risk Category 3 tier dominates and the most severe Category 1 sites remain a thin sliver.

Insight Early-childhood services (474) and government schools (331) hold about nine of every ten at-risk facilities. Yet the risk-tier mix barely shifts between sectors: Category 3 dominates every one, and the highest-risk Category 1 stays a thin sliver throughout.

Part 3

A century of fire

Knowing where today's schools sit on the map is only half the story. The Historical Bushfire Boundaries dataset records the outline of every officially mapped fire in Victoria since 1903 — more than 77,000 entries: 9,130 wild bushfires, and tens of thousands of planned prescribed burns lit by land managers to reduce fuel. Plotted year by year, the bushfire record is dominated by a small number of catastrophic seasons.

Most years barely lift off the axis — then a single summer leaps off the top of the chart. The 2003 alpine fires alone burnt more than the entire decade before them.

Average the footprints across whole decades and a second pattern emerges. The raw scale of the 2000s stands apart — but so does a quieter shift: prescribed burning, the fuel-reduction fires that land managers light on purpose, has grown decade on decade into the most common type of fire in Victoria by a wide margin.

Totals tell us how much burns — but not when. By month, two very different fire calendars emerge: one nature's, one ours.

Is that January peak new, or a permanent fixture of the Victorian summer? Stack the decades and see.

Insight The summer fire window — December through February — has been remarkably consistent for more than a century. What has changed is its intensity from one decade to the next.

The calendar of danger is the one thing a century of fires agrees on. When the next dangerous summer comes is predictable; how bad it will be is not.

Part 4

The shape of individual fires

Finally, the size of a fire matters as much as its season. Most bushfires are small — small enough to contain. What makes Victoria's record so dangerous is the long tail: every few decades a single fire grows large enough to reshape the state's geography. The 1939 Black Friday fires remain the largest single footprint on record, at roughly 2.4 million hectares.

Insight Most fires are small — the single most common size is just 1–10 hectares, and roughly seven in ten stay under 100 hectares. But the long tail is what makes seasons like 2009 catastrophic: only 53 recorded fires have ever reached 100,000 ha.

Insight Black Friday 1939 remains the largest single recorded footprint at 2.4 million hectares — almost twice the 2003 alpine event. The 2019–20 Black Summer is responsible for four of the 13 largest fires on record (Snowy Complex, Upper Murray–Walwa, Dunns Road and Tambo Complex), more than any other single season.

Read together, the two datasets give a stark answer: the map shows where today's children sit, and the history shows the kind of summer that can arrive at any time.

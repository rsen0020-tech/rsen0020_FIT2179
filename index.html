Victorian Bushfires

A century of fire — and the schools in its path.

Bushfire is woven into life in Victoria. Over more than a hundred years the state has burned and burned again, and each summer thousands of children sit in classrooms inside the highest-risk zones. This page brings together two public datasets — Victoria's Bushfire At-Risk Register of education facilities and a national record of historical fire boundaries — to show who is exposed today and how fire has shaped Victoria over the past century. It is written for everyday Victorians: parents, teachers, and anyone who wants to understand the risk where they live.

Part 1

Who is in the firing line today?

Victoria's Bushfire At-Risk Register lists 893 education facilities — from early-childhood centres to secondary schools — that sit in bushfire-prone areas, spread across 61 local government areas. The burden is far from even: it concentrates in the forested fringes of Melbourne and the state's east.

Figure 1. At-risk education facilities by LGA. Grey-to-red shading shows the count per area; alpine-resort and unincorporated facilities are not mapped.

Risk is highly concentrated: just three shires — Yarra Ranges, Greater Bendigo and Nillumbik — hold almost a third of every at-risk facility in the state.

The Central fire weather district — one of nine regions the Bureau of Meteorology uses to issue fire warnings — alone accounts for 473 facilities, and a single shire, Yarra Ranges in Melbourne's forested east, holds 156: more than twice any other.

Figure 2. Facilities by Bureau of Meteorology fire weather district.

Figure 3. The 15 local government areas carrying the most risk.

Most facilities sit in the register's lower tiers, but 24 are Category 1 — the highest-risk facilities, which must close pre-emptively when a Code Red day is declared. Early-childhood services are the single largest group on the register.

Part 2

A century of fire

The second dataset records more than 77,000 fire footprints in Victoria since 1903 — 9,130 of them bushfires, the rest planned prescribed burns lit to reduce fuel. Plotted over time, the bushfire record is dominated by a handful of catastrophic years.

Figure 7. Area burnt each year. Spikes mark Victoria's worst fire seasons.

A handful of seasons dominate the record: 2003, 2006, 2009 and the 2019–20 Black Summer together account for the great majority of the area burnt since 1950.

Those spikes line up with the seasons Victorians remember by name. Bushfires cluster tightly in summer, December to February, while prescribed burning is deliberately pushed into the cooler, calmer months of autumn and spring.

Figure 8. Area burnt per decade, bushfire vs planned burning.

Figure 9. Fires by month: two very different seasonal rhythms.

The size of fires is wildly uneven. Thousands burn only a few hectares, but a small number have scorched hundreds of thousands. The 1939 Black Friday fires remain the largest single footprint on record, at roughly 2.4 million hectares.

Victorian Bushfires

A century of fire — and the schools in its path.

Each summer, thousands of Victorian children sit in classrooms inside the state's highest bushfire-risk zones. This page joins two public datasets — the Bushfire At-Risk Register of education facilities, and a national archive of historical fire boundaries — to answer two questions for parents, teachers and anyone living in the bush interface: where is the risk concentrated today, and what kind of fire seasons should Victoria expect?

Part 1

Who is in the firing line today?

The Bushfire At-Risk Register is Victoria's roll-call of schools, kindergartens and early-learning centres that sit in bushfire-prone country. The 2025–26 edition lists 893 facilities, scattered across 61 of the state's 79 local government areas. The first thing the data shows is that this burden is anything but evenly spread.

Figure 1. Bushfire at-risk education facilities mapped to their LGA. Equal-area projection. Source: BARR register joined to ABS LGA boundaries.

Three shires alone — Yarra Ranges, Greater Bendigo and Nillumbik — hold almost a third of every at-risk facility in Victoria.

Step back from the map and a clear geography of risk emerges. The Bureau of Meteorology divides Victoria into nine fire weather districts — the regions used to issue Code Red and other warnings. Just one of them, the Central district that wraps around Melbourne's forested fringe, accounts for more than half of the register on its own. Among individual councils, Yarra Ranges stands out: it carries more than twice the at-risk count of any other shire.

Figure 2. Facilities by Bureau of Meteorology fire weather district.

Figure 3. The fifteen local government areas carrying the most risk.

Not every facility on the register faces the same level of danger. The register grades each one from Category 1 — the highest tier, where facilities must close pre-emptively on Code Red days — through Category 3, where exposure exists but is less acute. Most fall in the lower tiers, but the small handful of Category 1 sites carry an outsized share of the worry, and the mix differs sharply between districts.

Figure 5. How the risk-category mix differs across districts.

Risk also cuts across who runs each school. Early-childhood services dominate the register simply because there are so many of them, but the breakdown by education sector shows the same risk-tier mix repeated again and again.

Part 2

A century of fire

Knowing where today's schools sit on the map is only half the story. The second dataset — the Historical Bushfire Boundaries — records the outline of every officially mapped fire in Victoria since 1903. More than 77,000 entries: 9,130 wild bushfires, and tens of thousands of planned prescribed burns lit by land managers to reduce future fuel. Plotted year by year, the bushfire record is dominated by a small number of catastrophic seasons.

Figure 7. Total area burnt by Victorian bushfires (orange) and prescribed burns (green) each year, with the most destructive seasons labelled.

Four seasons — 2003, 2006, 2009 and the 2019–20 Black Summer — account for the great majority of the area Victoria has lost to bushfire since 1950.

Those spikes are the years Victorians remember by name. Average across the decades and a second pattern emerges: prescribed burning has grown into the dominant land-management activity, but it is deliberately scheduled into the safer shoulders of the year. Wild bushfires still arrive when they always have — in the hot, dry middle of summer.

Figure 8. Area burnt per decade, split between bushfires and planned burns.

Figure 9. Fires by month, side by side: two very different seasonal rhythms.

Stretching that monthly pattern across every decade in the record shows the summer fire window appearing in roughly the same shape, but with very different intensity from one decade to the next.

Figure 10. Bushfire count by month across the decades.

Finally, the size of a fire matters as much as its season. Most bushfires are small — small enough to contain. What makes Victoria's record so dangerous is the long tail: every few decades a single fire grows large enough to reshape the state's geography. The 1939 Black Friday fires remain the largest single footprint on record, at roughly 2.4 million hectares.

Figure 11. The distribution of recorded bushfire sizes — note the long tail.

Figure 12. The fifteen largest individual bushfires on record.

Read together, the two datasets give a stark answer to one question: who is exposed to fire in Victoria, and what kind of season can they reasonably expect? The map shows where today's children sit. The history shows the kind of summer that can arrive at any time.

About this story

Why this story matters

Bushfire is one of the defining risks of life in Victoria, and the question of who sits closest to that risk — particularly the state's youngest residents — has direct consequences for emergency policy, school closure procedures, and the everyday decisions of parents and teachers. This visualisation sets out to answer two questions for a general Victorian audience: where is bushfire risk to schools concentrated today, and what kind of fire seasons does Victoria's history tell us to expect?

Who it is for

The audience is the average Victorian — parents, teachers, school staff, and anyone who cares about how fire risk is distributed in their community. The narrative avoids statistical jargon, introduces specialised terms (Code Red days, prescribed burns, fire weather districts) at first use, and anchors every figure to something tangible: a shire, a season, a named fire.

What the data is

Two independent public datasets are joined:

  • Bushfire At-Risk Register (BARR) 2025–26, Victorian Department of Education, via data.vic.gov.au — 893 facilities tagged with risk category, fire weather district, education sector and local government area. This is the snapshot of present-day exposure.
  • Historical Bushfire Boundaries v2.0, Digital Atlas of Australia (Geoscience Australia), via digital.atlas.gov.au — every officially mapped Victorian fire since 1903 with ignition date, fire type and burnt area. This is the long historical record.
  • ABS Local Government Areas (ASGS 2011), filtered to Victoria and simplified for fast loading — the geographic base layer for the map.

The two source spreadsheets were aggregated into small derived tables in /data (a few hectares-per-LGA, fires-per-year, fires-by-month, and so on), keeping the page small and fast.

How it is visualised

The choice of visualisation idiom follows from the question each piece of data is meant to answer:

  • A choropleth map on an equal-area projection answers the geographic where. Position carries the LGA's identity; saturated colour carries the count of facilities, so the eye can compare regions of very different size without distortion.
  • Ranked horizontal bars answer "which is largest?". Bar length is the most accurate visual channel for quantitative comparison.
  • A stacked bar and a category × district heatmap answer "how does the mix differ?". Position carries the category; colour intensity carries the count.
  • A multi-series line chart with annotated event markers answers "how has this changed over time?", anchoring the reader with the named seasons of the last century.
  • A second heatmap (decade × month) reveals the persistent seasonal shape of the fire window beneath the year-to-year noise.
  • A histogram exposes the long-tailed size distribution of fires — the single most important fact about why Victoria's worst years are so damaging.

Colour is used semantically: a warm yellow-to-red sequential scale for fire-related intensity, a single green to separate planned burning from wild bushfire, and a soft warm cream for the editorial framing. Hover interaction adds detail-on-demand — tooltips on every chart, an LGA highlight on the map, and emphasis on hovered bars — without turning the page into an exploration tool.
